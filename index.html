The Invitation

Three generations ago, our family planted pine along this ridge above Kaliurang, where the air drops ten degrees the moment you leave the city behind. Van Resink is what grew from that planting — six houses set apart from one another by forest and footpath, built by hand from bamboo, volcanic andesite, and reclaimed teak.

There is no lobby to check into. A path leads you in from the road, past the spring, under the pines, to a house that is already yours for the night. We would love for you to walk it.

Explore

A ravine, a spring, and six hectares of pine

We designed Van Resink so you'd notice the mountain with all five senses, not just the view.

The property sits on a fold of land where the pine forest breaks open into a small ravine — Kali Kuning runs along the bottom of it, cold and clear straight off the volcano. Wild ginger and andaliman grow along the banks. Kingfishers work the shallows in the early morning, and the mist doesn't fully lift until well past nine.

Paths are uneven on purpose — hand-cut stone steps, a bamboo bridge that gives slightly underfoot, a stretch of boardwalk raised over the wettest ground. Nothing here is paved flat. There's always a reason to look down at what you're walking on, and up at what's growing over it.

The Spring Pool

A cold-water pool fed directly from the mountain spring, tucked into the ravine beside the river. No chlorine, no chemicals — just filtered volcanic stone and gravity.

Massage & Herbal Healing

Traditional Javanese lulur and steam treatments, given in an open-sided pavilion by therapists from the village below, using leaves gathered that same morning.

Yoga Pavilion

An open-air bamboo shala cantilevered over the irrigation channel, used for morning yoga, sound baths, and quiet mornings when nobody wants to talk yet.

Forest & Clean-Up Walks

A guided walk along the ravine to spot hornbills and macaques, with an optional detour: arm yourself with a bamboo spear and help us clear litter from the trailhead.

Garden & Harvest Mornings

Volcanic soil is poor but generous — chayote, kale, and andaliman all come from our own terraces. Join the morning harvest before it reaches your breakfast plate.

Bamboo & Timber Craft Tour

Visit the workshop of the three carpentry families who built every house on this slope, and see how a single bamboo culm becomes a roof beam.

The Path In

What you'll notice before you notice you've arrived

Every guest walks the same short route from the gate. It is designed to slow you down on purpose.

i.

The gate of pine — the road noise falls away first, then the heat.

ii.

The spring channel — cold mountain water, running beside the path in a hand-cut stone gutter.

iii.

The bamboo bridge — a short crossing over the ravine, built to flex, not to hide it.

iv.

The kitchen garden — chayote, kale, and andaliman brushing your ankles as you pass.

v.

The clearing — where the pines open and Merapi appears, usually capped in cloud.

vi.

Your house — door already unlatched, lamp already lit.

We keep walls to a minimum — most bathrooms are open to the sky, cooled by cross-ventilation rather than air conditioning. Every bed comes dressed in linen and draped in mosquito netting, with a hand-hammered copper basin in the bathroom and a rain shower fed by the spring. What we can't grow ourselves — coffee, honey, woven textiles — we buy from the village a ten-minute walk down the ridge.

A note on getting around: Wuni, Suren, and Kaliandra sit close to the gate on level ground. Puspa Nest, Merapi View Suite, and Wringin Cottage sit further down the ravine and involve stone steps and a short bamboo bridge — worth asking about if stairs are a concern.

Rates include use of the spring pool, kitchen garden, and morning coffee walk. Breakfast is cooked over open fire, à la minute.

Living with Merapi

Built to keep, not to impress

Merapi is one of the most active volcanoes in the world, and everything at Van Resink is built with that respect: light bamboo and timber frames, deep eaves, and foundations that can be repaired by hand, not machine.

We heat water with wood, grow what we can on the slope's poor volcanic soil anyway, and let the forest do most of the landscaping. What's left is a place that feels less designed than discovered.

Cool mountain air

No air conditioning needed above 800m — every house is cross-ventilated by design.

Spring-fed water

Drawn from the same channel that has watered this ridge for generations.

Bamboo & reclaimed teak

Grown or salvaged within 20km, worked by three local carpentry families.

No chemical treatment

Gardens and grounds kept the way the volcano keeps them — with compost and patience.

About Us

An accidental villa

We didn't set out to run a hotel. In the early 2000s, our family restored a single teak house on this ridge as a weekend escape from Yogyakarta — a place to leave the heat behind. Friends asked to stay. Then friends of friends. Eventually we restored a second house, then built a third from bamboo, and the weekend escape became Van Resink.

We still live down the road, which is part of why so little here has changed hands to contractors or design firms — every house has been built, fixed, and re-thatched by the same three families for almost two decades. We know the mountain because we never really left it.

We didn't design a hotel and land it on the mountain. We just kept building the same house we already loved, six times.
— The Van Resink family
